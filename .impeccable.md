# .impeccable.md

Persistent design context for Haven for Her. All design skills read this file before generating UI.

## Design Context

### Users

Haven for Her serves multiple audiences through a single application. Each audience encounters the product in a different emotional context:

- **Donors & supporters** — Philanthropic individuals, churches, and organizations discovering the nonprofit through social media, events, or word of mouth. They arrive wanting to help and need to feel confident their contribution matters. Context: desktop or mobile, often after seeing a social media post. Job: give money/time and understand the impact.
- **Survivors** — Young women in underserved urban US communities (starting South Side Chicago, expanding to NYC, Atlanta, Birmingham, LA) who are escaping cyclical abuse. They may be in crisis, on a borrowed phone, under stress. Context: mobile-first, potentially low bandwidth, high anxiety. Job: find safe help quickly, request counseling, access resources without friction or shame.
- **Counselors & volunteers** — Social workers, therapists, and community volunteers managing caseloads and documenting sessions. Context: desktop during working hours, task-oriented. Job: manage cases efficiently, take notes, track resident progress.
- **Admin & staff** — Executive leadership and operational staff overseeing finances, safehouses, and organization health. Context: desktop, data-driven. Job: make strategic decisions from dashboards, manage users and data.

**Critical UX principle:** The emotional tone shifts by audience. Donor-facing pages feel **inspiring and impact-driven** — show the difference their money makes. Survivor-facing pages feel **calm, safe, and low-pressure** — minimize cognitive load, use gentle language, never pressure or overwhelm. Staff/admin pages are **clear and efficient** while remaining warm.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** Nurturing. Trustworthy. Hopeful.

**Voice:** Warm, direct, and human. First-person plural ("We're here for you") rather than institutional third-person ("Services are available"). Empowering, never pitying — survivors are people rebuilding strength, not objects of charity. Every word earns its place.

**Emotional goals by context:**
- Public/donor pages → "I want to be part of this. These people are doing meaningful work."
- Survivor pages → "I feel safe here. This isn't going to overwhelm me."
- Staff dashboards → "I can trust this system. It helps me do my job well."

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone:** Soft, organic, and nurturing. The Bloom theme. Think therapy office waiting room — warm lighting, natural materials, plants — not hospital fluorescents.

**Theme:** Light mode only. Lavender-tinted backgrounds, cream card surfaces, sage green accents, plum headings. Rounded shapes everywhere. Generous whitespace. Fraunces serif for headings (warm, editorial), Nunito sans-serif for body (friendly, readable).

**Anti-references (explicitly avoid):**
- Corporate SaaS dashboards — cold grays, dense data grids, clinical feel
- Government / institutional sites — bureaucratic layouts, sterile palette, institutional typography
- Flashy startup marketing — neon accents, dark-mode heroes, gradient text, aggressive animations

**Reference mood:** A well-designed wellness app (Calm, Headspace) crossed with a premium nonprofit annual report. Approachable like a community bulletin board but polished like a modern product.

**Motion:** Moderate. Gentle page-load reveals, smooth hover transitions on cards/buttons, subtle entrance animations for sections as they scroll into view. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. No bounce, elastic, or attention-grabbing animation.

### Design Principles

1. **Safety first** — Every design choice should make the user feel safe, not sold to. Survivor-facing pages especially: low contrast CTAs, gentle language, clear escape paths, no pop-ups or aggressive modals.

2. **Warmth over precision** — When choosing between a mathematically perfect grid and a layout that feels more human and approachable, choose approachable. Organic shapes (rounded corners, flowing sections) over rigid geometry.

3. **Dignity in data** — Resident and case data must be presented with respect. Avoid clinical language in the UI. Use "Residents" not "Cases." Progress is shown as growth, not as metrics to be optimized.

4. **Clarity without coldness** — Staff dashboards need to be efficient and scannable, but they don't need to look like a Bloomberg terminal. Use the warm palette even for data-heavy pages. Tables and charts inherit the Bloom colors.

5. **Tailwind + shadcn, nothing else** — All styling through Tailwind utility classes and shadcn/ui component primitives. No inline styles, no custom CSS files, no CSS modules. Theme customization lives exclusively in CSS custom properties in `index.css`. This keeps the design system enforceable and consistent across all contributors and agents.
